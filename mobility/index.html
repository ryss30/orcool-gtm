The asymmetry

Your home team knows the brand. It does not know the market.

A brand grows into thirty countries but creative taste doesn't come with it. The people who built the winning angle at home have no feel for why someone in one market shares a bus and someone in another won't get in a stranger's car at all. So the home creative gets adapted down, translated, softened, and it lands as an outsider's guess. You find out it was wrong after the money is spent.

The other road is thirty local agencies, one per market, each with its own read on the brand and no shared standard between them. Now you're not scaling taste, you're managing chaos, and every market resets the clock. Either way the real number is the same one: how many days from market entry to a creative that clears your KPI gate and holds. That is Time-to-Winner, and both of these paths make it long.

Two sides, one market

One angle cannot start a two-sided market.

Supply and demand are two different sells. Recruiting drivers is a pitch about income, control, and whether the platform respects them. Recruiting passengers is a pitch about trust, price, and getting somewhere. Run one creative angle at both and you underserve both. The market needs a distinct, locally grounded angle for each side, built from what actually moves people there.

Supply is usually the harder wall. An empty market has no rides to sell until drivers show up, so the supply side is the real cold start, and it's the side the home creative ignores completely. The home-market passenger pitch, translated, does nothing for a driver in a new market deciding whether to sign up. Solve supply with its own local angle or the demand angle has nothing to point at.

Local Voice

Getting into a stranger's car is a local decision.

Trust here doesn't come from brand polish. It comes from local social norms, the ones that tell a person whether a stranger's seat is safe or whether letting someone book yours is normal. No amount of production value substitutes for a message that sounds like it was made by someone who lives there. Trust is the whole game in this category, and it's earned locally or not at all.

The moment a line reads as auto-translated, it reads as outsider, and in a message whose entire job is to build trust, that is fatal. You can't fix it with a reviewer signing off after the copy is written. The last mile has to be a real native speaker shaping the dialect and idiom from the inside, before anything ships.

Hausa runs near 70M speakers, Yoruba around 50M, Igbo about 44M, and no AI dubbing tool covers them properly today, so a real voice isn't a nice-to-have, it's the only way in.

Test fast at the cheap rung, move the winner up once the numbers earn it. The engine underneath is agnostic, so whatever wins, you're never locked to one vendor.

Questions

The honest answers.

Is this localization or transcreation?

Neither, exactly. Localization translates the home creative. Transcreation rewrites it for the local market but still starts from your idea. We start from local signals and let them pick the angle, so the winner can be something your home team would never have written. The Brand Lens keeps it yours.

How do you handle both sides of the marketplace at once, supply and demand?

As two separate problems with two separate angles. We source signals for the driver side and the passenger side independently, and you can set a separate KPI gate for each. Supply is usually the harder cold start, so it gets its own real angle, not a passenger pitch in disguise.

What about markets where AI voice doesn't work well?

Then we don't use it. Plenty of languages have no AI voice tool that passes as native, Hausa, Yoruba, and Igbo among them. In those markets a real native speaker records the voice. The ladder exists so you test cheaply where synthetic is fine and pay for a live voice only where the market demands it.

What does a pilot look like?

One market. We agree the KPI gate, source local signals for both sides, filter every candidate through your Brand Lens, lock the language with a native speaker, and put the winner into real spend. You judge us on Time-to-Winner and whether it holds. If it works, we expand to the next market on that proof.

What does it cost?

A pilot is scoped to one market, so it's a small, defined number, not an open-ended retainer. Pricing scales with how many markets you take live after the pilot proves out. We'll quote the pilot plainly once we know the market and the KPI gate. No card. No bullshit. Just start.
